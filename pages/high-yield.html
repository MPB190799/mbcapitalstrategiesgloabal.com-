High-Yield & BDCs

Business Development Companies, mortgage REITs, high-yield bonds, and income-focused strategies for yield-hungry investors.

Why High-Yield Income?

For investors seeking current income above what traditional fixed income provides, the high-yield universe offers a diverse set of instruments yielding 6-12% or more. These are not risk-free — the elevated yields compensate for real risks including credit losses, interest rate sensitivity, and NAV erosion. But for investors who understand these risks and size their positions appropriately, high-yield assets can form a powerful income engine within a diversified portfolio.

The key to success in high-yield investing is selectivity and diversification. A single high-yield position can suffer a permanent impairment. A well-constructed basket of 15-20 positions across different sub-sectors can deliver consistent income while absorbing the occasional loss.

Business Development Companies (BDCs)

BDCs are publicly traded companies that lend to middle-market businesses — companies too large for bank loans but too small for public bond markets. They are regulated under the Investment Company Act of 1940 and must distribute at least 90% of taxable income to shareholders, resulting in yields typically between 8-12%.

How BDCs Generate Income

BDCs earn the spread between their cost of borrowing and the interest rates they charge on loans to portfolio companies. A typical BDC might borrow at 5-6% (through institutional credit facilities, bonds, or notes) and lend at 10-13% (first-lien secured loans to middle-market companies). This 400-700 basis point spread, amplified by regulatory leverage of up to 2.0x debt-to-equity, generates the high returns that fund distributions.

Key BDC Metrics

  • Net Asset Value (NAV) — The fair value of a BDC's investment portfolio minus liabilities, expressed per share. BDCs trading below NAV offer a margin of safety; those above NAV require conviction in management's ability to generate outsized returns.
  • Net Investment Income (NII) — Interest and fee income minus operating expenses and interest costs. NII must cover the dividend — a BDC where NII consistently falls short of distributions is eroding NAV to pay shareholders.
  • Dividend Coverage — NII divided by dividends declared. Sustainable BDCs maintain coverage above 100%, ideally 105-115%, building spillover income that cushions against temporary earnings dips.
  • Non-Accrual Rate — Percentage of the portfolio on non-accrual status (loans where interest is no longer being collected). Below 2% of fair value indicates a healthy portfolio; above 5% signals potential credit problems.
  • Leverage Ratio — Debt-to-equity. Most BDCs operate at 1.0-1.2x, though regulations permit up to 2.0x. Higher leverage amplifies both returns and risks.
  • First Lien Percentage — The proportion of the portfolio in first-lien senior secured loans. Higher first-lien exposure means better recovery rates in defaults. Top BDCs maintain 70-90% first-lien portfolios.
  • Weighted Average Yield on Portfolio — The blended interest rate earned across all investments. This drives NII generation capacity.

BDC Red Flags

  • Persistent NAV decline over multiple quarters despite stable credit markets
  • Dividend coverage below 90% for two or more consecutive quarters
  • Rising non-accruals concentrated in a single industry or borrower
  • Excessive exposure to second-lien, subordinated, or equity co-investments
  • Related-party transactions with the external manager that disadvantage shareholders
  • Special dividends funded by unrealized gains rather than recurring income

Mortgage REITs

Mortgage REITs (mREITs) invest in mortgage-backed securities (agency and non-agency) and earn the spread between their borrowing costs (typically short-term repo financing) and the yield on their mortgage assets. This leveraged carry trade can produce yields of 10-15% but carries significant interest rate and spread risk.

Agency vs. Non-Agency mREITs

  • Agency mREITs — Invest in mortgage-backed securities guaranteed by Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, or Ginnie Mae. No credit risk, but highly sensitive to interest rate movements and the shape of the yield curve. Profits expand when the yield curve steepens and compress when it flattens or inverts.
  • Non-Agency/Credit mREITs — Invest in unguaranteed mortgage loans and securities. Carry credit risk but are less sensitive to interest rate movements. These tend to perform better during economic expansions when credit losses are low.
  • Hybrid mREITs — Combine agency and non-agency strategies. Diversification can reduce volatility relative to pure-play approaches.

Key mREIT Metrics

  • Book Value per Share — Equivalent to NAV. mREIT share prices tend to track book value over time.
  • Net Interest Spread — Portfolio yield minus funding cost. The driver of earnings.
  • Leverage — Debt-to-equity, typically 5-8x for agency mREITs and 2-4x for credit mREITs. Higher leverage amplifies both income and book value volatility.
  • Hedging Effectiveness — How well the mREIT hedges against interest rate movements. Unhedged positions in a rising rate environment can destroy book value rapidly.
  • Economic Return — Dividend yield plus/minus book value change. The true measure of total return for mREIT shareholders.

High-Yield Bonds & Funds

High-yield (or "junk") bonds are fixed-income instruments rated below investment grade (below BBB- by S&P or Baa3 by Moody's). They compensate investors for elevated default risk with higher coupon rates. For most individual investors, high-yield exposure is best accessed through diversified funds rather than individual bonds.

Closed-End Funds (CEFs)

Closed-end funds trade on exchanges at prices that can deviate significantly from their net asset value. A CEF trading at a 10% discount to NAV effectively gives you $1.00 of assets for $0.90, boosting your effective yield. Many high-yield CEFs use moderate leverage (25-35% of assets) to amplify income, producing distribution rates of 8-11%.

The key advantage of CEFs over open-end funds is that they never face forced selling due to redemptions. This allows managers to hold illiquid positions through downturns rather than selling at distressed prices, which benefits long-term shareholders.

Evaluating High-Yield Funds

  • Distribution rate vs. earnings yield — Some funds pay distributions from return of capital rather than earnings. Check the Section 19(a) notices to understand the source of distributions.
  • Discount/Premium to NAV — Buy at wider-than-average discounts, avoid paying premiums. Historical discount ranges provide context.
  • Leverage ratio — Structural leverage amplifies both income and losses. Ensure the fund can maintain its leverage even during credit market stress.
  • Credit quality distribution — The mix of BB, B, CCC, and unrated holdings. Higher CCC exposure means higher yield but also higher default risk.
  • Expense ratio — Including management fees and leverage costs. High expenses are a persistent drag on total returns.

Income Strategy Construction

Building a high-yield income portfolio requires balancing yield with risk management:

  1. Diversify across sub-sectors — Spread allocations across BDCs, mREITs, CEFs, and direct high-yield to avoid concentration in a single risk factor
  2. Size positions conservatively — No single high-yield position should exceed 3-5% of the income portfolio. A catastrophic loss in one name should not materially impair total portfolio income.
  3. Ladder maturities and rate sensitivity — Mix floating-rate assets (BDCs, leveraged loans) with fixed-rate holdings (bonds, preferred shares) to reduce interest rate directionality
  4. Monitor coverage ratios relentlessly — A distribution cut is the greatest risk in high-yield investing. Watch NII coverage, non-accrual trends, and book value stability quarterly.
  5. Reinvest selectively — Rather than automatically reinvesting all distributions, accumulate cash during rich valuations and deploy aggressively when discounts widen during market stress
  6. Accept that some positions will fail — Even the best high-yield portfolio will experience occasional permanent capital losses. The goal is to earn enough income from the portfolio as a whole to absorb these losses while still generating attractive total returns.
