Sector Analysis

Energy & Upstream

Oil and gas producers, production economics, and reserve analysis — the foundation of hard-asset energy investing.

Why Upstream Energy?

Upstream oil and gas companies explore for and produce the hydrocarbons that power the global economy. Despite decades of "peak oil" predictions, the world still consumes over 100 million barrels of oil per day, and global natural gas demand continues to set records. For income-oriented investors, the upstream sector offers a compelling combination of high current yields, commodity price leverage, and tangible asset backing.

The upstream sector transformed dramatically after the 2014-2016 oil price collapse and the 2020 pandemic crash. Surviving producers abandoned the growth-at-all-costs model that defined the shale revolution's first decade. Today, the dominant strategy among public E&Ps is capital discipline: moderate production growth, aggressive shareholder returns, and balance sheet strength. This philosophical shift has turned previously speculative growth stocks into reliable income generators.

Production Economics

Understanding the economics of oil and gas production is fundamental to evaluating upstream investments. Every barrel produced has an associated cost structure that determines whether it generates profit or loss at prevailing prices.

Break-Even Analysis

An upstream company's break-even price is the commodity price at which its operations generate zero free cashflow. This figure incorporates:

  • Lifting costs (LOE) — The direct cost of extracting hydrocarbons from existing wells, including labor, power, chemicals, and workovers. Typically $5-15/boe for conventional producers, $8-20/boe for offshore.
  • Gathering, processing, and transportation (GP&T) — Fees paid to midstream operators to move product from the wellhead to sales point. Ranges from $2-8/boe depending on basin and infrastructure availability.
  • Production taxes and royalties — Severance taxes (state-level) and royalty payments to mineral rights owners. These typically represent 15-25% of revenue.
  • General and administrative (G&A) — Corporate overhead allocated per barrel. Efficient operators keep this below $3/boe.
  • Capital expenditures — The cost of drilling new wells to replace declining production. This is the largest variable and the primary determinant of break-even economics.
  • Interest expense — Debt service costs. Companies with heavy leverage face significantly higher break-evens.

The Decline Curve

Every oil and gas well declines in production over time as reservoir pressure depletes. Understanding decline rates is critical because they determine how much capital a company must reinvest simply to maintain flat production.

  • Conventional wells — Typically decline 5-15% per year. Mature conventional assets can produce for decades with minimal reinvestment.
  • Shale/tight oil wells — First-year decline rates of 60-75% are common, stabilizing to 10-15% in subsequent years. This means shale producers must drill continuously to offset steep initial declines.
  • Corporate decline rate — The blended decline rate of a company's entire production base. Lower is better — it means less capital is needed for maintenance.

Reserve Analysis

Reserves are the lifeblood of an upstream company. They represent the estimated quantity of hydrocarbons that can be economically extracted under current conditions. Reserves are categorized by the confidence level of their estimation.

Reserve Categories

  1. Proved Developed Producing (PDP) — Reserves from wells that are currently producing. The highest certainty category and the most relevant for valuation.
  2. Proved Developed Non-Producing (PDNP) — Reserves from wells that have been drilled but are not yet producing (shut-in wells, behind-pipe zones). Require minimal additional capital to bring online.
  3. Proved Undeveloped (PUD) — Reserves on undrilled locations that are adjacent to producing wells and can be recovered with reasonable certainty. Require significant capital investment. SEC rules require PUDs to be developed within five years or reclassified.
  4. Probable and Possible — Lower-confidence estimates used primarily by Canadian companies (2P and 3P reserves). These provide upside optionality but should not be relied upon for valuation.

Key Reserve Metrics

  • Reserve life index (RLI) — Total proved reserves divided by annual production. An RLI of 10+ years indicates a deep inventory that supports sustained production without aggressive drilling.
  • Reserve replacement ratio (RRR) — Annual reserve additions divided by annual production. Consistently above 1.0x demonstrates organic reserve replacement.
  • Finding and development cost (F&D) — Capital spent on drilling and completions divided by reserves added. Lower F&D costs indicate superior capital efficiency. Top-tier Permian operators achieve F&D costs below $10/boe.
  • PV-10 — The present value of estimated future net revenues from proved reserves, discounted at 10%. This is the SEC-standardized measure of reserve value and provides a floor valuation for upstream companies.

Key Basins

Permian Basin (Texas/New Mexico)

The most prolific oil-producing basin in the Western Hemisphere. Multiple stacked pay zones (Wolfcamp, Bone Spring, Spraberry) provide decades of drilling inventory. Operators here benefit from the lowest break-even costs in North America, robust infrastructure, and proximity to Gulf Coast export facilities.

Appalachian Basin (Marcellus/Utica)

The dominant natural gas producing region in the United States. Marcellus and Utica shale wells are among the most productive gas wells ever drilled, with exceptionally low break-even costs. Growing LNG export demand and data center power needs are creating structural demand tailwinds for Appalachian gas producers.

DJ Basin (Colorado)

A prolific oil and gas basin in the Niobrara and Codell formations. Regulatory risk from Colorado's evolving permitting framework has historically created a valuation discount relative to other basins, presenting opportunity for investors who can underwrite the regulatory environment.

Canadian Oil Sands

Among the largest hydrocarbon reserves on earth, with reserve life measured in decades rather than years. Oil sands operations have high initial capital costs but very low decline rates — once a mine or SAGD facility is built, it can produce for 25-40+ years. The completion of the Trans Mountain Pipeline expansion has improved Canadian heavy oil price realizations.

What We Look For

  1. Low break-even prices — Companies that generate free cashflow even at $55/barrel WTI ensure portfolio resilience through downturns
  2. Return of capital frameworks — Explicit policies that allocate a defined percentage of free cashflow (typically 60-80%) to dividends and buybacks
  3. Deep inventory — 10+ years of high-quality drilling locations at current activity levels prevents the need for value-destroying acquisitions
  4. Low corporate decline rates — Below 25% annually means less capital is consumed simply maintaining production
  5. Conservative leverage — Net debt/EBITDA below 1.0x. The best operators today are approaching net cash positions.
  6. Hedging discipline — Protecting a portion of near-term production through put options or costless collars stabilizes cashflows without capping all upside

Risks to Monitor

  • Commodity price volatility — Oil and gas prices can move 30-50% in a single year. Upstream earnings amplify these swings in both directions.
  • Inventory exhaustion — Some prolific basins are maturing. Companies running low on tier-one locations may face rising costs or need to make acquisitions.
  • Regulatory and ESG pressure — Evolving regulations on emissions, flaring, and permitting can increase costs and constrain activity.
  • OPEC+ policy — Production decisions by OPEC+ member nations can overwhelm supply-demand fundamentals in the short term.
  • Geopolitical disruption — Conflicts, sanctions, and political instability in producing regions create both upside and downside surprises.
