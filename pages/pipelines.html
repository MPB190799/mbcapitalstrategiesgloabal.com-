Sector Analysis

Pipelines & Midstream

Master Limited Partnerships, fee-based cashflows, and the toll-road infrastructure that moves North American energy.

Why Midstream?

Midstream companies occupy the most defensible position in the energy value chain. While upstream producers face commodity price risk and downstream refiners battle crack spread volatility, midstream operators collect fees for gathering, processing, transporting, and storing hydrocarbons. Think of them as toll roads for molecules — they get paid regardless of whether oil is at $50 or $100 per barrel.

This fee-based business model produces remarkably stable cashflows that support some of the highest yields in the equity market. Many midstream companies distribute 5-9% annually, and the best operators grow those distributions steadily while maintaining conservative coverage ratios.

The MLP Structure

Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs) were the original midstream investment vehicle. They pass through income directly to unitholders, avoiding corporate-level taxation. While many MLPs have converted to C-corps in recent years for index inclusion and broader investor access, understanding the MLP structure remains essential for midstream investors.

Key MLP Characteristics

  • K-1 Tax Reporting — MLPs issue Schedule K-1 forms rather than 1099s. Distributions are generally treated as return of capital, deferring taxes until units are sold. This creates significant tax advantages for long-term holders.
  • Distribution Coverage Ratio (DCR) — Distributable Cash Flow (DCF) divided by actual distributions paid. A DCR above 1.2x indicates a well-covered distribution with room for growth. Below 1.0x signals the partnership is paying out more than it earns — a red flag.
  • Incentive Distribution Rights (IDRs) — Legacy structures where the general partner received an increasing share of incremental distributions. Most have been eliminated through simplification transactions, removing a major drag on unitholder returns.
  • UBTI Concerns — MLP income in tax-advantaged accounts (IRAs, 401ks) can trigger Unrelated Business Taxable Income above $1,000. This makes MLPs best held in taxable accounts for most investors.

Contract Structures

The quality of a midstream company's revenue depends entirely on its contract structure. Not all midstream cashflows are created equal.

Take-or-Pay Contracts

The gold standard. Under take-or-pay agreements, the shipper commits to a minimum volume and must pay the reservation fee whether or not they actually ship product. These contracts provide guaranteed minimum revenue regardless of throughput, effectively insulating the midstream operator from short-term volume declines. Look for companies where 80%+ of revenue comes from take-or-pay or minimum volume commitment (MVC) contracts.

Fee-Based vs. Commodity-Exposed

  • Pure Fee-Based — Revenue tied solely to volumes transported or processed, with zero direct commodity price exposure. The most predictable model.
  • Fee-Based with Escalators — Fees that increase annually with inflation (CPI or PPI-linked). Provides built-in organic growth without capital investment.
  • Percent-of-Proceeds (POP) — The midstream operator receives a percentage of the commodity sold. Creates direct commodity price exposure — higher upside but more volatile.
  • Keep-Whole — The processor keeps the extracted NGLs and returns dry gas to the producer. Exposes the midstream company to the spread between NGL prices and natural gas prices.

Midstream Sub-Sectors

Natural Gas Pipelines

Interstate natural gas transmission pipelines are regulated by FERC and earn returns based on allowed rate cases. These are among the most utility-like assets in the midstream space, with long-term contracts (often 10-20 years) and high barriers to entry. The ongoing build-out of LNG export capacity along the Gulf Coast and data center power demand are creating sustained demand growth for gas transportation.

NGL Pipelines & Fractionation

Natural Gas Liquids (ethane, propane, butane, natural gasoline, isobutane) must be separated from raw natural gas and transported to petrochemical plants. Fractionation — the process of separating the NGL stream into individual purity products — is concentrated at a handful of large facilities along the Gulf Coast. Operators with fractionation capacity benefit from high utilization rates and long-term supply agreements.

Crude Oil & Refined Products Pipelines

Crude gathering systems in producing basins like the Permian, Bakken, and SCOOP/STACK connect wellheads to larger trunk lines. Refined product pipelines move gasoline, diesel, and jet fuel from refineries to distribution terminals. These systems benefit from relatively inelastic demand — people drive and fly regardless of moderate price swings.

Gathering & Processing

The closest midstream assets to the wellhead. Gathering systems collect raw natural gas and crude oil from individual wells and deliver them to processing plants or larger pipeline systems. G&P assets carry more volume risk than long-haul pipelines because they depend on continued drilling activity in their dedicated acreage. However, they often command higher returns precisely because of this risk.

Key Metrics for Midstream Analysis

  • Distributable Cash Flow (DCF) — Net income plus depreciation and amortization, minus maintenance capital expenditures. The cash actually available to distribute to unitholders or shareholders.
  • Distribution Coverage Ratio (DCR) — DCF divided by distributions paid. We target companies with a DCR of 1.3x or higher for a margin of safety.
  • EV/EBITDA — The standard valuation metric for midstream. Historically, large-cap midstream has traded at 8-12x EV/EBITDA. Below 8x often signals value; above 12x warrants caution.
  • Debt/EBITDA — Leverage ratio. Investment-grade midstream companies typically target 3.5-4.5x. Above 5.0x represents elevated financial risk, especially during downturns.
  • Contract Mix — Percentage of revenue from fee-based vs. commodity-exposed contracts. Higher fee-based percentages indicate more predictable cashflows.
  • Counterparty Quality — The creditworthiness of the shippers who have contracted pipeline capacity. Investment-grade counterparties reduce the risk of contract renegotiation or default.
  • Growth Capex Backlog — Permitted and contracted expansion projects that will drive future EBITDA growth. A visible pipeline of projects at attractive build multiples (typically 4-7x EBITDA) signals years of ahead distribution growth.

What We Look For

When evaluating midstream investments for our portfolio, we prioritize:

  1. Fee-based revenue above 85% — Minimizes commodity price sensitivity and supports reliable distributions through all market environments
  2. Distribution coverage of 1.3x or better — Provides a substantial buffer before distributions would need to be cut, even during volume declines
  3. Investment-grade credit rating — Ensures access to capital markets at reasonable rates and reduces refinancing risk during credit crunches
  4. Self-funding growth model — Companies that can fund expansion projects from retained cashflow and debt, without dilutive equity issuance
  5. Basin diversification — Exposure to multiple producing regions reduces the risk that a single-basin production decline devastates the entire portfolio
  6. Management alignment — Insider ownership and compensation structures tied to per-unit metrics rather than aggregate growth

Risks to Monitor

Midstream is not without risk. Investors should monitor:

  • Regulatory and permitting risk — New pipeline construction faces increasing environmental opposition and permitting delays. Projects can be cancelled after billions in spending.
  • Volume risk in declining basins — If producers reduce drilling activity in an operator's dedicated acreage, gathering volumes can fall materially.
  • Interest rate sensitivity — Midstream yields compete with fixed income. Rising rates can compress midstream valuations even when fundamentals remain strong.
  • Contract roll-off — When long-term contracts expire, renewals may come at lower rates if competitive dynamics have changed.
  • Energy transition — Long-term decline in fossil fuel demand could impair the useful life of midstream assets, though natural gas infrastructure may benefit from the transition period.
