Sector Analysis

Mining Sector

Gold, copper, coal, and critical minerals — understanding AISC, royalty streams, and the art of investing in cyclical commodity producers.

Why Mining?

Mining companies extract the physical resources that underpin modern civilization — from the copper wiring in every building to the lithium in every battery. For hard-asset investors, miners offer direct leverage to commodity prices with the potential for extraordinary returns when cycles turn favorable.

The mining sector is intensely cyclical. Years of underinvestment in new supply create scarcity, which drives prices higher, which eventually incentivizes new development — but with lead times of 7-15 years from discovery to production. This structural lag between price signals and supply response creates persistent boom-bust dynamics that disciplined investors can exploit.

Sub-Sectors

Gold Miners

Gold miners provide leveraged exposure to gold prices. When gold rises 20%, a well-run miner with fixed costs can see earnings expand by 50-100% or more. The key metric is All-In Sustaining Cost (AISC), which captures the full cost of maintaining current production levels. Miners with AISC below $1,200/oz generate substantial free cashflow at current gold prices, supporting both dividends and share buybacks.

The gold mining industry has undergone a dramatic transformation since the reckless expansion of 2010-2012. Today's senior producers focus on capital discipline, returning cash to shareholders, and maintaining reserve replacement through targeted exploration rather than value-destroying mega-acquisitions.

Copper Producers

Copper is the metal most essential to the energy transition. Electric vehicles use 3-4x more copper than internal combustion engines. Renewable energy systems require 4-5x more copper per megawatt than fossil fuel generation. The electrification thesis creates a structural demand story that could last decades.

On the supply side, copper faces serious challenges. Average ore grades at existing mines have declined from over 1.5% in the 1990s to roughly 0.6% today, meaning miners must move far more rock to produce the same amount of copper. New greenfield projects face 10-15 year development timelines and escalating capital costs. The resulting supply gap is a central thesis for copper bulls.

Metallurgical & Thermal Coal

Coal remains one of the most misunderstood sectors. While thermal coal faces long-term structural decline in developed markets, metallurgical coal (used in steelmaking) has no commercially viable substitute at scale. Global steel production continues to grow, driven by infrastructure development in emerging economies.

Meanwhile, thermal coal producers in underinvested regions have become exceptional cashflow generators. Starved of capital by ESG-driven divestment, surviving operators face limited competition and benefit from elevated prices while returning enormous amounts of cash to shareholders through dividends and buybacks.

Royalty & Streaming Companies

Royalty and streaming companies represent the highest-quality business model in the mining sector. Rather than operating mines themselves, they provide upfront capital to miners in exchange for a percentage of future production (royalties) or the right to purchase a percentage of production at a fixed, below-market price (streams).

This model delivers critical advantages:

  • No operating cost exposure — Royalty companies do not pay for mining, processing, or transportation. Their margins expand as commodity prices rise while remaining protected on the downside.
  • Built-in diversification — A single royalty company may hold hundreds of royalties across dozens of commodities and jurisdictions.
  • Optionality — Exploration-stage royalties may become valuable producing assets at zero additional cost.
  • Low G&A burden — Royalty companies operate with tiny teams relative to their asset bases, keeping overhead minimal.

Understanding AISC

All-In Sustaining Cost (AISC) is the single most important metric for evaluating mining companies. Introduced by the World Gold Council in 2013 and now adopted across the industry, AISC captures the true cost of maintaining production at current levels.

AISC Components

  1. Cash costs — Mining, processing, and site-level G&A expenses
  2. Royalties and production taxes — Payments to governments and landowners
  3. Sustaining capital expenditures — Capital required to maintain current production (stripping, tailings management, equipment replacement)
  4. Corporate G&A — Head office overhead allocated per ounce/pound
  5. Exploration expense — Sustaining exploration to replace depleted reserves

What AISC does not include is growth capital — spending on new mines or major expansions. This is why AISC alone is insufficient; you must also examine total capital expenditure and free cashflow to understand the full picture.

Cycle Timing

Mining is one of the most cyclical industries in existence. Understanding where you are in the cycle is often more important than individual company analysis.

  • Late-cycle downturn — Commodity prices below production costs, mines shuttering, companies cutting dividends and slashing exploration budgets. This is when the best long-term investments are made.
  • Early recovery — Prices rise above marginal cost of production, surviving companies generate cashflow, balance sheets begin to heal. Share prices start moving but most investors remain skeptical.
  • Mid-cycle expansion — Rising prices attract capital, companies restart mothballed projects and begin exploration. Dividends are initiated or increased. This is the sweet spot for total returns.
  • Late-cycle euphoria — Record commodity prices, massive capex commitments on marginal projects, M&A premiums balloon, analysts project prices rising forever. This is when disciplined investors reduce exposure.

Key Metrics for Mining Analysis

  • AISC per ounce/pound — Measures cost competitiveness relative to peers and commodity prices
  • Reserve life — Proven and probable reserves divided by annual production. Longer reserve life reduces reinvestment risk.
  • Replacement ratio — Annual reserve additions divided by annual production. Above 1.0x means the company is growing its reserve base.
  • Free cashflow yield — Operating cashflow minus all capex, divided by market capitalization. Mining companies should be evaluated on FCF, not earnings.
  • Jurisdiction risk — The political and regulatory environment where mines are located. Stable jurisdictions (Canada, Australia, parts of the US) command valuation premiums.
  • Net debt/EBITDA — Leverage ratio. Mining companies should operate conservatively (below 1.5x) given the cyclicality of their revenues.
  • Sustaining capex intensity — Sustaining capex as a percentage of revenue. Lower is better, as it indicates more cashflow available for dividends and growth.

What We Look For

  1. First-quartile cost position — Mines in the lowest cost quartile survive downturns and thrive in upturns
  2. Long reserve life (10+ years) — Reduces the need for constant reinvestment in new projects
  3. Proven management track record — Operators who have navigated prior downturns without destroying shareholder value
  4. Conservative balance sheets — Low leverage ensures survival through the trough
  5. Shareholder return policies — Explicit dividend frameworks tied to free cashflow, ideally with a base dividend plus variable component
  6. Favorable jurisdiction — Low political risk, established mining law, and stable fiscal regimes
